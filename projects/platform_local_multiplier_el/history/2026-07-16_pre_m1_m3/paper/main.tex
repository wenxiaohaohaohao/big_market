\title{Market Integration, Platformization, and Local Multipliers:\\
The Spatial Reorganization of Value Capture}
\author{Wenxiao Dong \and Yaqi Hu}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study how market integration and platformization reorganize where
transaction value is captured. In a small open region, integration lowers the
platform channel's price and may expand autonomous local income, but shifts
spending toward an intermediary whose local-income share depends on local
organizational capacity. We solve the income fixed point. Closed-form
thresholds show when access gains dominate capture losses and when consumer
gains coexist with falling local income. Stronger local organization raises
effective multipliers and can reverse adverse integration effects. When the
platform retains less value locally, capture drag peaks before platform
penetration reaches one-half.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} market integration; digital platforms; local
multipliers; value capture; spatial incidence

\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} F15; L81; R11; R12

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Market integration expands access to products and markets, but digital
platforms also reorganize the intermediaries through which transactions are
matched, settled, and fulfilled. Expenditure in a region need not become
income there: platform fees, seller profits, and service revenue may accrue
elsewhere. The local effect therefore depends on both market-access gains and
the share of transaction value captured locally. Because captured income
finances demand for local services, this spatial incidence also changes the
effective local multiplier.

Evidence from rural China shows that e-commerce access can lower living costs
without generating comparable gains for local producers and workers
\citep{couture2021connecting}. Online trade changes regional consumption and
real wages \citep{fan2018alibaba}, and retail entry can generate price gains
alongside local labor and profit effects \citep{atkin2018retail}.

The local-multiplier literature studies how tradable-employment or
fiscal-spending shocks propagate across local activity
\citep{moretti2010local,chodorowreich2019geographic}. Retail channel
organization, however, is not the object of analysis in these studies. With
platforms, otherwise identical expenditure flows need not generate the same
local income: seller margins, fulfillment revenue, platform payments, and
ownership returns may accrue outside the region. Local propagation therefore
depends on both respending and the transaction value retained in each round.

We introduce this channel-specific retention margin into a small-open-region
multiplier framework. Integration lowers the relative platform price and may
expand autonomous base income, while endogenous channel substitution changes
the average share of expenditure captured as local labor, service, and locally
owned business income. Organizational capacity \(\kappa\) shifts platform
retention between lower and upper bounds; it does not require local platform
ownership. The model yields closed-form access--capture thresholds for nominal
and consumption-equivalent real income and ordered capacity thresholds: weak
local organization can make capture losses dominate, intermediate capacity can
preserve consumer gains despite falling local income, and strong capacity can
make access gains dominate while raising the effective multiplier. For a fixed
capacity level with \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\), marginal capture drag is strictly
hump-shaped and peaks before the platform share reaches one-half. This is a
short-run local equilibrium, not a model of platform pricing or a multi-region
quantitative spatial equilibrium.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

Consider a small region \(L\) embedded in an outside economy \(H\). Channel
\(P\) is an outside digital-platform channel whose relative price responds to
market integration; \(A\) is a local-agent channel.

\begin{assumption}[Primitives]
\label{ass:primitives}
Let \(z\in\mathbb R\) index the degree of market integration; a higher \(z\)
denotes stronger integration and a lower platform-channel trade cost.
\(
\alpha,\beta>0
\), \(
\alpha+\beta<1
\), \(
a\in(0,1)
\), \(
\eta>1
\), and
\[
 p_A,\bar p_P,\bar B>0,\quad
 G,\varepsilon\geq0,\quad \rho_A\in[0,1],\quad \kappa\in[0,1],
\]
\[
 0\leq\underline\rho_P<\overline\rho_P\leq1,\qquad
 \delta_\rho\equiv\overline\rho_P-\underline\rho_P>0.
\]
The channel-\(P\) price is \(p_P(z)=\bar p_Pe^{-z}\), and autonomous base
income is
\(B(z)=\bar Be^{\varepsilon z}\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Local-income closure]
\label{ass:closure}
The outside-good price and local wage are one. A competitive constant-returns
sector produces the nontradable with \(N=L_N\), so its price is \(p_N=1\);
labor or capacity is elastically supplied at its fixed opportunity cost. Each dollar
spent through channel \(c\) generates \(\rho_c\) dollars of local labor,
service, and locally owned business income; the remainder accrues to \(H\).
Local organizational capacity \(\kappa\) changes only the spatial incidence of
platform transactions, not prices or preferences. Autonomous base income \(B\)
is local value added outside the modeled retail and nontradable sectors and
excludes income generated by \(G\). Residents own all local income and share
the preferences below. Finally, \(G\) is retail expenditure funded from
outside \(L\) and allocated across channels by \(s(z)\). Each induced spending
round purchases newly produced output and creates income according to these
incidence shares.
\end{assumption}

We treat \(L\) as small and take the platform-price schedule \(p_P(z)\) as
exogenous to the region: integration moves the price along this schedule,
whereas local income and expenditure do not feed back into it. We take the
outside good as numeraire and assume that the fixed opportunity cost of
elastically supplied local labor equals one in those units. Because the
competitive nontradable technology uses only local labor, \(N=L_N\), zero
profit implies \(p_N=w=1\), so all nontradable revenue is local labor income.
These restrictions isolate channel-specific transaction incidence as the
model's spatial-capture margin; \(\rho_c\) is therefore a total local-income
share, not necessarily a labor-cost share.

A representative resident solves
\begin{equation}
 \max_{M,N,Z\geq0} M^\alpha N^\beta Z^{1-\alpha-\beta}
 \quad\text{s.t.}\quad P_MM+N+Z=Y.
 \label{eq:outer-problem}
\end{equation}
Retail consumption combines a local-agent channel \(A\) and platform channel
\(P\):
\begin{equation}
 M=\left[(1-a)^{1/\eta}M_A^{(\eta-1)/\eta}
 +a^{1/\eta}M_P^{(\eta-1)/\eta}\right]^{\eta/(\eta-1)}.
 \label{eq:channel-ces}
\end{equation}
Conditional on \(M\), the household minimizes
\begin{equation}
 \min_{M_A,M_P\geq0}\{p_AM_A+p_P(z)M_P\}
 \quad\text{s.t. \eqref{eq:channel-ces}.}
 \label{eq:inner-problem}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Household and channel demand]
\label{lem:demand}
For \(Y>0\), both problems have unique interior solutions. Expenditure on
\((M,N,Z)\) is \((\alpha Y,\beta Y,(1-\alpha-\beta)Y)\). Moreover,
\begin{align}
 P_M(z)&=\left[(1-a)p_A^{1-\eta}
 +ap_P(z)^{1-\eta}\right]^{1/(1-\eta)}, \label{eq:price-index}\\
 M_c&=a_c\left(\frac{p_c}{P_M}\right)^{-\eta}M,
 \quad (a_A,a_P)=(1-a,a), \label{eq:hicksian-demand}\\
 s(z)&=\frac{ap_P(z)^{1-\eta}}
 {(1-a)p_A^{1-\eta}+ap_P(z)^{1-\eta}}\in(0,1). \label{eq:platform-share}
\end{align}
For every finite \(z\),
\begin{equation}
 \frac{d\ln P_M}{dz}=-s,\qquad
 \frac{ds}{dz}=(\eta-1)s(1-s)>0.
 \label{eq:access-derivatives}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Platform transactions retain the share
\begin{equation}
 \rho_P(\kappa)=\underline\rho_P+\kappa\delta_\rho,
 \label{eq:platform-retention}
\end{equation}
where \(\kappa\) summarizes the local seller, fulfillment, and service
organization able to participate in platform trade. Average local value
capture is
\begin{align}
 \omega(z,\kappa)&=(1-s)\rho_A+s\rho_P(\kappa),
 \label{eq:retention}\\
 \omega_z&=-\Delta(\kappa)(\eta-1)s(1-s),\qquad
 \omega_\kappa=s\delta_\rho>0,
 \label{eq:retention-derivatives}\\
 \Delta(\kappa)&\equiv\rho_A-\rho_P(\kappa).
\end{align}
The benchmark condition \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\) is imposed only where stated; a
lower platform retention share is not part of the definition of a platform.

Residents spend \(\alpha Y\) through the retail channels and \(\beta Y\) on the
local nontradable. With \(X_A=(1-s)(\alpha Y+G)\) and
\(X_P=s(\alpha Y+G)\), Assumption~\ref{ass:closure} gives nontradable revenue
\(p_NN=N=\beta Y\) and channel income
\(\rho_AX_A+\rho_P(\kappa)X_P=\omega(\alpha Y+G)\).

\begin{definition}[Local-income equilibrium]
\label{def:equilibrium}
For given \(z\), \(\kappa\), and \(G\), an equilibrium is
\[
(M_A,M_P,M,N,Z,s,\omega,Y,R)
\]
that satisfies household optimization, channel expenditure minimization,
\eqref{eq:retention}, and
\begin{equation}
 Y=B(z)+\beta Y+\omega(z,\kappa)[\alpha Y+G],
 \label{eq:income-fixed-point}
\end{equation}
 with \(R\) the consumption-equivalent real-income index implied by the
 household problem. The objects \(P_M\) and \(D\) are derived uniquely from
 \(z\) and the primitives.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Existence and uniqueness]
\label{lem:equilibrium}
An equilibrium exists, is unique, and has \(Y>0\). For fixed \(z\), the income
map
\[
 T(Y)=B+\omega G+(\beta+\alpha\omega)Y
\]
is a contraction on \(\mathbb R_+\); iteration from any \(Y_0\geq0\) converges
to
\begin{equation}
 Y(z,\kappa,G)=\frac{B(z)+\omega(z,\kappa)G}{D(z,\kappa)},\qquad
 D(z,\kappa)=1-\beta-\alpha\omega(z,\kappa)>0.
 \label{eq:income-solution}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Indeed, \(0\leq\beta+\alpha\omega\leq\alpha+\beta<1\).
Household optimization then gives consumption-equivalent real income
\begin{equation}
 R(z,\kappa,G)=K\frac{Y(z,\kappa,G)}{P_M(z)^\alpha},\qquad
 K=\alpha^\alpha\beta^\beta
 (1-\alpha-\beta)^{1-\alpha-\beta}.
 \label{eq:real-income}
\end{equation}
This is an indirect consumption index conditional on Assumption~\ref{ass:closure},
not social welfare including labor supply and opportunity costs.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\begin{proposition}[Local-income and gross-spending multipliers]
\label{prop:multiplier}
Holding \(z\), \(\kappa\), and \(G\) fixed, treat realized \(B\) as an
independent autonomous-income injection (equivalently, vary its level shifter
\(\bar B\)).
\begin{align}
 m_B(\omega)&\equiv\frac{\partial Y}{\partial B}
 =\frac{1}{1-\beta-\alpha\omega},
 \label{eq:local-income-multiplier}\\
 \mathcal M_G(\omega)&\equiv\frac{\partial Y}{\partial G}
 =\frac{\omega}{1-\beta-\alpha\omega}
 =\omega m_B(\omega)
 =\omega\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}(\beta+\alpha\omega)^k.
 \label{eq:recursive-multiplier}
\end{align}
Let \(m_0=(1-\alpha-\beta)^{-1}\). Then
\(\mathcal M_G(\omega)\leq m_0\omega\), with equality exactly at
\(\omega\in\{0,1\}\) and strict inequality for \(0<\omega<1\). For
\(\omega>0\),
\begin{equation}
 \frac{\partial\ln\mathcal M_G}{\partial\ln\omega}
 =\frac{1-\beta}{1-\beta-\alpha\omega}>1.
 \label{eq:retention-elasticity}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The multiplier \(m_B\) propagates one dollar already formed as local income.
The gross-spending multiplier \(\mathcal M_G\) additionally applies first-round
incidence \(\omega\). Its series shows why \(m_0\omega\) overstates propagation:
the latter applies leakage only initially, whereas retail leakage recurs when
induced income finances new retail expenditure.

Set \(G=0\), take \(z_1>z_0\), let
\(B_j=B(z_j)\), \(D_j=D(z_j,\kappa)\), and
\(P_{Mj}=P_M(z_j)\) for \(j\in\{0,1\}\), and define
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal B=\frac{B_1}{B_0},\qquad
 \mathcal D=\frac{D_1}{D_0},\qquad
 \mathcal P=\frac{P_{M1}}{P_{M0}}.
 \label{eq:finite-objects}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Finite access--capture thresholds]
\label{prop:thresholds}
Fix \(\kappa\). Suppose \(G=0\) and \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\). Then
\(\mathcal D>1\), \(0<\mathcal P<1\), and
\begin{equation}
 \frac{Y_1}{Y_0}=\frac{\mathcal B}{\mathcal D},\qquad
 \frac{R_1}{R_0}=
 \frac{\mathcal B}{\mathcal D\mathcal P^\alpha}.
 \label{eq:finite-ratios}
\end{equation}
The three strict regions are: (i) both incomes rise if
\(\mathcal B>\mathcal D\); (ii) nominal income falls but real income rises if
\(\mathcal D\mathcal P^\alpha<\mathcal B<\mathcal D\); and (iii) both fall if
\(\mathcal B<\mathcal D\mathcal P^\alpha\). At
\(\mathcal B=\mathcal D\), nominal income is unchanged and real income rises;
at \(\mathcal B=\mathcal D\mathcal P^\alpha\), nominal income falls and real
income is unchanged. These cases are exhaustive; nominal income cannot rise
while real income falls.
\end{proposition}

Two consequences are useful. Since \(\mathcal B\geq1\), holding
\(\mathcal D\) and \(\mathcal P\) fixed
while allowing \(\varepsilon\geq0\), the joint-loss region is nonempty if and
only if \(\mathcal D\mathcal P^\alpha>1\). At
\(\varepsilon=0\), the local condition
\(\Delta(\kappa)(\eta-1)(1-s)>D(s,\kappa)\) makes both marginal effects
negative.

For marginal changes at \(G=0\), define
\begin{equation}
 \Lambda(s,\kappa)=
 \frac{\alpha\Delta(\kappa)(\eta-1)s(1-s)}
 {D(s,\kappa)}.
 \label{eq:substitution-drag}
\end{equation}

\begin{corollary}[Organizational capacity and integration]
\label{cor:marginal}
Suppose \(G=0\). Then
\begin{equation}
 \frac{d\ln Y}{dz}=\varepsilon-\Lambda(s,\kappa),\qquad
 \frac{d\ln R}{dz}=\varepsilon+\alpha s-\Lambda(s,\kappa).
 \label{eq:marginal-effects}
\end{equation}
Moreover,
\begin{align}
 \frac{\partial\ln Y}{\partial\kappa}
 &=\frac{\alpha s\delta_\rho}{D}>0, \label{eq:capacity-income}\\
 \frac{\partial\Lambda}{\partial\kappa}
 &=-\frac{\alpha\delta_\rho(\eta-1)s(1-s)
 (1-\beta-\alpha\rho_A)}{D^2}<0, \label{eq:capacity-drag}\\
 \frac{\partial\ln\mathcal M_G}{\partial\kappa}
 &=\frac{(1-\beta)s\delta_\rho}{\omega D}>0
 \quad\text{if }\omega>0. \label{eq:capacity-multiplier}
\end{align}
Let \(h(s)=(\eta-1)(1-s)\) and
\(d_A=1-\beta-\alpha\rho_A\). For \(q\geq0\) satisfying
\(\Lambda(s,0)>q>\Lambda(s,1)\), the condition implies \(q<h(s)\); define
\begin{equation}
 \kappa(q)=\frac{1}{\delta_\rho}
 \left[
 \rho_A-\underline\rho_P-
 \frac{q\,d_A}{\alpha s[h(s)-q]}
 \right].
 \label{eq:capacity-threshold}
\end{equation}
This is the unique capacity in \((0,1)\) at which
\(\Lambda(s,\kappa)=q\). If
\(\Lambda(s,0)>\varepsilon+\alpha s>\varepsilon>\Lambda(s,1)\), then
\[
 0<\kappa_R^*\equiv\kappa(\varepsilon+\alpha s)
 <\kappa_Y^*\equiv\kappa(\varepsilon)<1.
\]
Both marginal effects are negative below \(\kappa_R^*\); only real income
rises between the two thresholds; and both rise above \(\kappa_Y^*\).
\end{corollary}

The two access gains are \(\varepsilon\) for nominal income and
\(\varepsilon+\alpha s\) after adding the consumer-price effect. Organizational
capacity lowers the opposing capture drag and increases the multiplier. If
\(\rho_P(\kappa)\geq\rho_A\), then \(\Lambda\leq0\), so integration cannot
reduce either income measure under \(\varepsilon\geq0\).

\begin{corollary}[Intermediate-penetration peak]
\label{cor:peak}
Fix \(\kappa\) with \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\), and let
\(d_A=1-\beta-\alpha\rho_A\). The function
\(s\mapsto\Lambda(s,\kappa)\), extended continuously to \([0,1]\), is strictly
concave and has the unique maximizer
\begin{equation}
 s^\dagger(\kappa)=
 \frac{1}{1+\sqrt{1+\alpha\Delta(\kappa)/d_A}}<\frac12.
 \label{eq:penetration-peak}
\end{equation}
Writing \(t(\kappa)=\sqrt{1+\alpha\Delta(\kappa)/d_A}\), its maximum is
\begin{equation}
 \Lambda_{\max}(\kappa)
 =(\eta-1)\frac{t(\kappa)-1}{t(\kappa)+1}.
 \label{eq:maximum-drag}
\end{equation}
Over interior capacity levels for which \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\),
\(\partial s^\dagger/\partial\kappa>0\) and
\(\partial\Lambda_{\max}/\partial\kappa<0\); the corresponding feasible
one-sided derivatives apply at \(\kappa\in\{0,1\}\).
\end{corollary}

The peak is a marginal, not cumulative, effect. Platform substitution is weak
near either channel-share boundary, while stronger local organization reduces
the retention gap at every interior share. Figure~\ref{fig:mechanisms}
illustrates both implications with an admissible parameterization satisfying
the endpoint-crossing condition; it is not a calibration.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Market integration can expand access while platformization relocates the
income generated by transactions. The model separates these forces and shows
how the retained share governs both first-round income formation and subsequent
local demand. Low organizational capacity can make capture losses dominate;
intermediate capacity can preserve consumer gains despite lower local income;
and sufficiently high capacity can make access gains dominate while raising
the effective multiplier. When \(\Delta(\kappa)>0\), the marginal capture drag
is greatest during intermediate platform penetration, before either channel
dominates. The framework is deliberately a short-run local equilibrium: it
takes the platform-price schedule and production locations as given, abstracts
from tax incidence, and evaluates consumption-equivalent real income rather
than full social welfare. These restrictions isolate channel-specific retention
as the link between platform intermediation and local income formation;
embedding this mechanism in a multi-region equilibrium is a natural extension.
