## Review Body

### Strongest rejection case

After defining omega as the expenditure-weighted average of two channel coefficients, nominal income is a familiar geometric induced-spending multiplier. Calling omega endogenous because CES changes the channel share changes a coefficient in the existing mechanism. Proposition 1's first-round and induced-round distinction is standard Type-II practice. Proposition 2 defines sign regions from the same income ratios. The apparently distinctive below-one-half peak is generated by the CES share derivative.

The paper is therefore a polished worked example: a price decline shifts expenditure toward a channel assumed to retain less local income; the standard multiplier falls; consumer-price gains may offset nominal loss; and the CES marginal drag is hump-shaped. Mathematical validity survives. The current novelty claim does not.

### Functional-form challenge

For any differentiable share path s(z), with G equal to zero:

- omega(s) = rho_A - Delta-rho times s;
- D(s) = 1 - beta - alpha rho_A + alpha Delta-rho times s;
- general drag = alpha Delta-rho times s-prime(z), divided by D(s).

This general expression does not imply a unique interior peak or a peak below one half. A variable-elasticity share path can move the maximum to either side, create multiple maxima, or make the drag monotone. A substantive theorem should state conditions on the first and second derivatives of the share path; CES should be a corollary.

### Prior-art challenge

- [Miyazawa (1960)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-pdf/74/1/53/5206190/74-1-53.pdf) combines input-output analysis and household consumption feedback.
- The official [BEA RIMS II User Guide](https://www.bea.gov/sites/default/files/methodologies/RIMSII_User_Guide.pdf) defines Type-II multipliers with induced household spending, separates first and subsequent rounds, and treats purchases outside the region as leakage.
- [Round (1985)](https://academic.oup.com/ej/article-abstract/95/378/383/5190759) analyzes multiplier decomposition with regional and world trade.

These sources do not show that the exact below-one-half formula already exists. They require a formal comparison that identifies what price-induced channel composition adds beyond Type-II/SAM/Miyazawa accounting.

### Defenses that survived

- The mathematics is internally correct under CES.
- The coefficient ordering is explicitly conditional and the reverse case is reported.
- R is labeled an indirect consumption index rather than full welfare.
- The G-equals-zero restriction is visible and the appendix contains general-G finite derivatives and ratios.
- The source and PDF are polished and readable.

### Minimum repairs

1. CRITICAL: establish a theorem unavailable in Type-II/SAM/Miyazawa comparative statics, or add a genuinely endogenous spatial-incidence margin.
2. MAJOR: derive the drag for a general share function and make CES a corollary.
3. MAJOR: microfound or discipline the channel-coefficient difference through seller margins, fulfillment labor, delivery, platform fees, ownership, and local production.
4. MAJOR: replace ratio-defined thresholds with primitive, elasticity, or observable conditions and demonstrate meaningful magnitudes.
5. MAJOR: extend the central comparative statics to positive and integration-dependent G, or narrow the application.
6. MAJOR: state one falsifiable payoff unavailable from recomputing a standard regional multiplier after expenditure shares change.
7. MINOR: retain the welfare and spatial-equilibrium disclaimers.
